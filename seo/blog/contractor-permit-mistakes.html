10 Permit Mistakes Contractors Make (And How to Avoid Them)

The permitting process can feel like a maze of paperwork and regulations. For busy contractors, it's easy to make a misstep that can lead to costly delays, failed inspections, and unhappy clients. Here are the ten most common permitting mistakes and how your business can avoid them.

1. Assuming a Permit Isn't Needed

The Mistake: Thinking a "simple" or "like-for-like" replacement (like a water heater or HVAC system) doesn't require a permit.
The Reality: Nearly all replacements of major systems require a permit because codes change. A new installation must meet today's standards for safety and efficiency.
How to Avoid: When in doubt, always assume you need a permit. A five-minute call to the building department or a quick check on their website can save you from a major headache.

2. Submitting an Incomplete Application

The Mistake: Leaving fields blank, forgetting to include equipment model numbers, or not providing a clear scope of work.
The Reality: An incomplete application is the #1 cause of permit delays. It goes to the bottom of the pile until you provide the missing information.
How to Avoid: Create a pre-application checklist for your team. Ensure every required piece of information—owner details, license numbers, equipment specs, scope of work—is ready before you start filling out the form.

3. Incorrectly Calculating Fees

The Mistake: Using an old fee schedule or miscalculating the project valuation, leading to underpayment and a hold on your permit.
The Reality: Cities update their fee schedules regularly. A quote based on last year's fees can hurt your profit margin.
How to Avoid: Bookmark the official fee schedule for each city you work in. Double-check it for every single job before finalizing a quote for the customer.

4. Not Having the Permit On-Site

The Mistake: The work is permitted, but the permit card or approved plans are not physically present at the job site for the inspection.
The Reality: The inspector will not—and cannot—conduct the inspection without the official permit card. This results in an automatic failure and a re-inspection fee.
How to Avoid: Make it a company policy. The moment a permit is issued, it gets placed in a permit box or a designated, visible spot on site. No work begins until the permit is posted.

5. Scheduling the Inspection Prematurely

The Mistake: Calling for an inspection before the job is 100% ready, hoping to "get in the queue."
The Reality: If the work isn't complete or accessible, the inspector will fail the job within minutes. This wastes their time and costs you a re-inspection fee.
How to Avoid: Use a "Ready for Inspection" checklist. Have your lead technician or project manager physically walk the site and sign off that every item on the pre-inspection list is complete before scheduling the call.

6. Ignoring Plan Review Comments

The Mistake: For larger jobs, receiving a plan review with required corrections and then failing to implement them before or during the installation.
The Reality: The inspector's checklist is based on those approved plans. If your installation doesn't match, the inspection will fail.
How to Avoid: The project manager must review all plan corrections with the installation crew before work begins. Any changes should be clearly marked on the field documents.

7. Forgetting to Close Out the Permit

The Mistake: Completing the work, getting paid, and moving on without ensuring the final inspection passed and the permit is officially closed.
The Reality: An open permit can create a huge problem for the homeowner years later when they try to sell or refinance their property. It reflects poorly on your business.
How to Avoid: Implement a final closeout procedure in your project management system. The final payment from the client should not be processed until you have confirmation of a passed final inspection.

8. Letting a Permit Expire

The Mistake: Starting a job and then having it stall for months due to client delays or other issues, causing the permit to expire.
The Reality: Once a permit expires, you often have to start the entire application process over again, sometimes paying the full fee.
How to Avoid: Track permit expiration dates (usually 180 days after issuance or the last inspection). If a project is stalled, perform a minor inspection if possible to keep the permit active, or apply for a formal extension before it expires.

9. Using the Wrong Terminology in the Scope of Work

The Mistake: Writing "repairing sewer line" when you are actually "replacing" a 10-foot section.
The Reality: Vague or incorrect wording can trigger a more complex plan review or cause issues during inspection. "Repair" often has a different definition in the code book than "replace" or "alter."
How to Avoid: Be specific and use code-accurate language. "Replacing 40-gallon gas water heater with new 50-gallon gas unit in same location" is much better than "water heater swap."

10. The Homeowner Pulling the Permit

The Mistake: Allowing a homeowner to pull the permit in their name to "save money."
The Reality: The person who pulls the permit is legally responsible for ensuring the work meets code. If a homeowner pulls the permit, they become the general contractor, and you lose control and assume significant liability if something goes wrong.
How to Avoid: Make it a strict company policy that you pull all permits for your own work. Explain to the homeowner that this is for their protection as well as yours, as it ensures a licensed, insured professional is taking responsibility for the project.
