Do You Need a Permit to Upgrade an Electrical Panel?

Of all the skilled trades, electrical work carries some of the highest risks. The main electrical panel is the heart of a building's electrical system, and any work on it is a major undertaking. Therefore, the answer to this question is an unequivocal and universal YES. A permit is always required.

The Unbreakable Rule: Any replacement, upgrade, or relocation of a main electrical service panel requires an electrical permit from the local jurisdiction. There are virtually no exceptions to this rule anywhere in the United States.

Why Panel Upgrades are a Critical, Permitted Job

An electrical panel upgrade is far more than just swapping a box on the wall. It involves disconnecting the entire building from the power grid and making new, high-amperage connections that must be perfect. The reasons for requiring a permit and inspection are numerous:

  • Extreme Fire Hazard: A poorly made connection on the main service lugs can create an arcing fault, generating thousands of degrees of heat and causing a catastrophic fire. An inspector verifies these connections are torqued to specification.
  • Utility Coordination: The job requires the utility company to disconnect and reconnect power at the weatherhead. Most utilities will refuse to re-energize a service without a passed inspection and a green tag from the city.
  • System Grounding: The grounding electrode system is the primary safety mechanism for the entire building. An inspector will ensure it is correctly sized and bonded according to the latest National Electrical Code (NEC).
  • Code Compliance: The NEC is updated every three years. A panel upgrade is the opportunity to bring the service up to modern standards, which may include new requirements for AFCI/GFCI protection, surge protection, or labeling.
  • Ensuring Proper Sizing: An upgrade (e.g., from 100A to 200A) must be accompanied by correctly sized service entrance conductors. A permit process ensures this calculation is done and verified.

The Process for a Permitted Panel Upgrade

Because of the inherent risks and coordination involved, a panel upgrade permit process is more involved than for a simple circuit addition.

  1. Permit Application: The licensed electrical contractor submits a detailed application, often including a simple diagram (a "one-line") of the new service configuration.
  2. Utility Work Order: The contractor coordinates with the local electric utility to schedule a "disconnect/reconnect" (D&R).
  3. Installation: On the scheduled day, the utility disconnects the power. The contractor then removes the old panel and meter base, installs the new equipment, and completes all wiring.
  4. Inspection: The city electrical inspector arrives on-site the same day to conduct a thorough inspection of the new panel, grounding, and connections.
  5. Re-energization: Once the inspector approves the work and places a sticker on the new panel, they notify the utility. The utility company then returns to reconnect the power.

This entire process, from power-down to power-up, typically happens within a single business day.

What About Replacing Breakers?

While a full panel replacement always requires a permit, what about simply replacing a circuit breaker?

  • Like-for-Like Replacement: Replacing a standard 20A breaker with another standard 20A breaker of the correct type for the panel does not require a permit.
  • GFCI/AFCI Upgrade: Replacing a standard breaker with a code-required GFCI or AFCI breaker does not require a permit.
  • Changing Amperage: Changing a breaker to a higher amperage (e.g., 15A to 20A) is an alteration of the circuit's design and does require a permit because the wire gauge must be verified.

The Dangers of Unpermitted Panel Work

Attempting to bypass the permit process for a panel upgrade is exceptionally dangerous and foolish.

  • The utility company will likely refuse to reconnect power without an inspector's approval, leaving the building without electricity.
  • It creates a massive, uninsurable liability for both the electrician and the property owner.
  • It puts the building and its occupants at a significant risk of electrical fire.
  • A home inspector will immediately flag an unpermitted panel during a future sale, potentially killing the deal.

For licensed electricians, performing unpermitted panel work is one of the fastest ways to lose a license and face legal action. There is no shortcut when it comes to the heart of the electrical system.
