HVAC Permit Requirements in Austin, TX (2025 Guide)

Navigating the world of HVAC permits in Austin can be complex for even seasoned contractors. The City of Austin Development Services Department (DSD) has specific rules that must be followed to ensure compliance, safety, and efficiency. This guide breaks down everything you need to know for 2025.

When is an HVAC Permit Required in Austin?

In Austin, a mechanical permit (which covers HVAC) is required for most installation and replacement work. According to the Austin City Code, a permit is necessary for:

  • New HVAC System Installation: Any new installation of a heating, ventilation, or air conditioning system in a new or existing building.
  • Complete System Replacement: Replacing an entire existing HVAC system, including the condensing unit and the air handler/furnace.
  • Relocation of Equipment: Moving an existing HVAC unit, such as a condenser, to a new location.
  • Alterations to Ductwork: Significant changes or additions to the ductwork system. Minor repairs do not typically require a permit.

Work Exempt from an HVAC Permit

Not all HVAC work requires a permit. The city exempts minor repair and maintenance work, which includes:

  • Routine maintenance like cleaning coils or checking refrigerant levels.
  • Replacing minor components with like-for-like parts (e.g., a fan motor, capacitor, or thermostat).
  • Repairing small sections of ductwork.

The key distinction is "replacement" versus "repair." If you're replacing a major component like a condenser or air handler, a permit is almost always required.

The Austin HVAC Permitting Process

The permitting process in Austin is handled online through the Austin Build + Connect (AB+C) portal. As a licensed contractor, you must be registered with the city to pull permits.

1. Application Submission

All applications are submitted digitally. You will need to provide detailed information, including:

  • Project address and owner information.
  • A clear scope of work, detailing the equipment being installed (make, model, SEER rating).
  • For larger projects, you may need to submit a Manual J calculation to demonstrate proper system sizing.
  • Your Texas HVAC contractor license number (TACLA/B).

2. Plan Review

For most simple HVAC change-outs, the permit can often be issued over-the-counter or through a simplified review process. More complex installations, especially those involving new construction or significant alterations, will undergo a more thorough plan review by the DSD. This can take several business days to a few weeks, depending on workload.

3. Permit Fees

Permit fees in Austin are based on the valuation of the work. As of early 2025, you can expect the following (but always verify with the official fee schedule):

Work Valuation Typical Permit Fee
$0 - $2,000 ~$85.00
$2,001 - $20,000 ~$150.00 + percentage of valuation
$20,001+ Varies significantly

Disclaimer: Fees are subject to change. Always consult the official City of Austin Development Services Department fee schedule before quoting a project.

Inspections: The Critical Final Step

Once the work is complete, you must schedule an inspection. The inspector will verify that the installation complies with the current Mechanical Code adopted by the city (typically the International Mechanical Code with local amendments).

What Inspectors Look For:

  • Proper Equipment Placement: Condenser clearances, proper drainage for the evaporator coil.
    • - Correct Sizing: Verification that the equipment and ductwork are appropriately sized for the structure.
  • Refrigerant Lines: Proper insulation and support.
  • Condensate Drain: Correct slope, cleanouts, and an emergency drain pan with a float switch.
  • Electrical Connections: Safe and code-compliant electrical disconnects and wiring.
  • Ductwork: Sealed and insulated ductwork to prevent energy loss.

Failing an inspection can lead to costly delays and rework. It's crucial to perform the installation to code standards the first time. Scheduling is also done through the AB+C portal.

Why Pulling a Permit Matters

Skipping the permit process might seem like a way to save time and money, but it can lead to serious consequences for both you and the homeowner, including fines, legal liability, and difficulty selling the property. For a contractor, it can jeopardize your license. Always follow the rules to protect your business and your clients.
